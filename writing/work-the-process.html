"Work the Process"

I had a thermodynamics professor in college who took one of the first days of class to teach us the 12 step problem solving process he insisted we use on every assignment and exam.

Thanks to my wife, I was able to recreate what the steps were:

On a piece of engineering paper, write the following things in order:

  1. Your name
  2. The full problem statement
  3. The 'given' facts
  4. What you've been asked to find
  5. The thermodynamic system involved
  6. The physical system involved
  7. The fundamental equations you will use
  8. The assumptions you are making
  9. The type of process involved
  10. Your working equations
  11. Physical properties or constants
  12. The solution

A majority of the credit came from steps 1-11; the final answer mattered, but less than whether you worked each step of the process correctly.

This wasn't an attempt at grade inflation or to make people feel like they were still winners even if they got the answer wrong. It was because of a genuine belief that working a defined process in engineering was the path to long-term success. If you can set up the basics correctly - identify the problem, clarify what you know and what assumptions you are making, etc. - you will more likely than not be able to get the right answer. More importantly, you'll be able to do this consistently for the thousands of problems that you'll face over the course of your career.

We all mocked the professor's instructions to "work the process" whenever someone asked a question, but more and more in the years since that class, I've come to realize that "working the process" applies in almost any context:

  • Want to improve your marketing site performance? Design, run, and analyze a sequence of experiments, and chances are you'll end up improving performance.
  • Trying to tackle a difficult feature? Decide what you want it to do, write some failing tests, make the tests pass, and before you know it you'll have finished the detailed feature.
  • Want to lose weight? Figure out how many calories you take in and how many calories you expend, and adjust one or both of those to create a deficit. Before long you'll lose weight.
